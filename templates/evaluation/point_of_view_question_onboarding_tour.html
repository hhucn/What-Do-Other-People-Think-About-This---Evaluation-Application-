  • @MC A vaccine provides “instructions” to the body’s immune systems to enable the immune system response to the invading microorganism to be swifter and more specific. The person’s body does the work - all the vaccine provides is a “picture” (antigen) that resembles the real microorganism. Like many things in life, vaccines may not provide the perfect “picture” (in this case, the virus is changing constantly), and not all people may be able to “recognize” the “picture”, so some vaccines may not be perfectly functional for every single recipient. There is a separate type of passive immunity (antiserum) where antibodies made by a separate individual are injected into the individual at risk, in order to provide an immediate response. An example is the rabies antiserum (immunoglobulin) - the bitten person’s own immune system simply cannot respond quickly enough to stop the rabies virus, which kills rapidly.
